# Van road trip from Capbreton to San Sebastián: four days to taste the Basque coast

Too often, we rush towards Spain. The motorway swallows the coast, you arrive in San Sebastián worn out, you leave having seen nothing. This travel diary proposes the opposite: four days in a van to link Capbreton to San Sebastián, wandering, stopping at the right addresses, letting the van take the rhythm of the tides.

The itinerary: Capbreton, Biarritz, Saint-Jean-de-Luz, Hondarribia, Zarautz, San Sebastián. Two hundred kilometres in all, which you deliberately stretch out. Tested with Peggy, the workshop's Fiat Ducato L2H2, rattan and sage-green dinette, perfect for four people in galley-on-board mode.

## Day 1: Capbreton to Biarritz, easing in

Leave the workshop late morning, time enough to fill the water tank and swing by Capbreton's covered market for supplies. South on the D810, the road shadowing the Adour. Only thirty kilometres, but you take your time.

Stop at Bidart, at the viewpoint of the Chapelle Sainte-Madeleine. From there, the Basque coast opens up, the waves breaking in long lines on Ilbarritz beach. Smell of tamarisk, warm wind carrying the scent of heated eucalyptus.

In Biarritz, park Peggy at the Lac Marion motorhome aire, €12 a night, shaded, full services. For dinner, head to Chez Albert on the fishermen's port: grilled fish, squid in its ink, a terrace facing the boats. Budget around €25 per person.

At the end of the day, drop down to the Côte des Basques for the evening surf session. It is the historic birthplace of European surfing. Even without a board, watching the longboards glide in silhouette against a sun setting over Spain is reason enough to come.

## Day 2: Biarritz to Saint-Jean-de-Luz, the corniche

A quiet start. Coffee at Bar Jean in Biarritz, a 1950s counter the regulars still frequent. Then take the D912, the Basque corniche, fifteen kilometres or so through Guéthary and Bidart.

A compulsory stop on the coastal path between Guéthary and Saint-Jean-de-Luz. An hour and a half of walking above the cliffs, gorse and heather, the sea fifty metres below. The sound of waves breaking on rock is deeper here than elsewhere. You hear it before you see it.

In Saint-Jean-de-Luz, park at the Fort de Socoa car park, on the Ciboure side, free and tolerated overnight out of season. Direct view of the bay. For lunch, drop into Pâtisserie Adam, rue de la République, for a traditional Basque macaron and a gâteau basque with black cherry. The shop has been doing this since 1660, without interruption.

In the evening, aperitif on the port, stroll along the beach at dusk. The October light is pale pink, almost powdery.

## Day 3: crossing into Spain, Hondarribia and Zarautz

Break camp early. The border at Hendaye passes without checks most of the time, but keep ID cards and vehicle registration to hand. As soon as you cross the Santiago bridge, you are in Hondarribia, and everything changes: red roofs, painted facades, Basque on every sign.

Climb up to the fortified old town. On Calle San Nikolas, push open the door of Gran Sol, a local institution that has been serving avant-garde pintxos for thirty years. A glass of txakoli, two pintxos, around €10.

Then west on the N634, avoiding the A8 toll road. The road winds, climbs, drops. Stop at Zarautz, a long straight beach, a mythical surf spot. Van parking tolerated on the beachfront, eastern side, out of season. In summer, the Gran Camping Zarautz higher up is a better bet, with a view over the bay.

Late lunch at Karlos Arguiñano on the seafront, more upmarket, or simpler at Txiki Polit in the town centre, for a set lunch at €15. Plate of Iberian ham, grilled peppers, fish of the day.

The light falls late in summer, the beach stays warm until midnight. Teenagers play football barefoot. You can hear the pelotaris at the nearby fronton, the dry crack of the ball on the wall.

## Day 4: Zarautz to San Sebastián, the pintxos

Final leg, short, only twenty kilometres. But you leave the main road to pass through Getaria, a fishing village clinging to its rock. Park at the bottom of the town, visit on foot. Restaurant Elkano, if the budget allows, for a turbot grilled over wood fire. Otherwise, the plancha txipirones at Bar Astillero are enough to understand the region.

Arrival in San Sebastián in the early afternoon. Park the van at El Antiguo, west of the city, a tolerant residential zone. For the night, the official aire Donostia San Sebastián Camper Park, a little further out, is the best option: €12, services, shuttle bus to the centre.

In the afternoon, ride the funicular up Monte Igueldo for the view over the Concha bay in its perfect arc. In the evening, head to the Parte Vieja for the pintxo crawl. Bar Txepetxa for anchovies, Borda Berri for veal cheeks, La Cuchara de San Telmo for pan-fried foie. Count €3 per pintxo, €2 per glass.

The smell of grilled octopus, vinegar, sidra poured from the top of the barrel into the glass. Crowd noise, laughter, Basque and Spanish mingling.

## Practical advice for the road

Border: no systematic check between Hendaye and Irun, but papers always within reach. Fuel: fill up on the Spanish side, noticeably cheaper, around 15 cents less per litre in 2025. Currency: euro on both sides, but many small pintxo bars do not take card under €10, keep cash on you.

Parking: in Spain, the term to look for is "área autocaravana". Useful app: Park4Night, with a "van" filter. Avoid unauthorised parking in tourist centres, fines in San Sebastián reach €150.

Water: Spanish aires are fewer but well equipped. Fill up completely in Saint-Jean-de-Luz before the border.
